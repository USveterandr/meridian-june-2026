The Dominican Republic is one of the most foreigner-friendly real estate markets in the hemisphere. Foreign nationals have the same property rights as Dominican citizens — you can own land, structures, and title outright, with no restrictions. Yet many potential buyers still hesitate because they don't know how the process works.

This guide walks you through every step.

---

## Can Foreigners Buy Property in the Dominican Republic?

Yes — completely and unreservedly. The Dominican Republic's constitution guarantees equal property rights for nationals and foreigners. There is no foreign ownership cap, no need for a local partner, and no restrictions on which types of property you can purchase (residential, commercial, agricultural, or coastal).

This makes the DR one of only a handful of countries in the Caribbean and Latin America where you can own freehold title as a non-citizen.

## Step 2: Hire a Dominican Attorney (Notary)

This is not optional. DR real estate law requires a notary (notario público) to validate and register the transaction. A good attorney will:

- Conduct a title search at the Registro de Títulos (Title Registry)
- Verify there are no liens, mortgages, or encumbrances
- Confirm the seller's legal right to sell
- Draft the purchase agreement (Promesa de Venta / Contrato de Compra-Venta)
- Handle the registration transfer after closing

Attorney fees: typically 1–2% of the purchase price. On Meridian, you can find verified Dominican lawyers and notaries listed on the platform.

---

## Step 3: Title Search & Due Diligence

Before signing anything, your attorney requests a Certificado de Título (Certificate of Title) from the Registro de Títulos. This is the official government record of ownership. The search confirms:

- Who legally owns the property
- Whether there are any mortgages or liens
- Zoning classification (residential, tourism zone, etc.)
- Boundary confirmations

This process takes 1–3 weeks. Do not skip it. The DR has had historical cases of informal land claims (especially in coastal areas) — a proper title search eliminates this risk.

---

## Step 4: Sign the Purchase Agreement

Once due diligence clears, you sign a Promesa de Venta (Promise of Sale). This is a legally binding contract that:

- Specifies the price, payment schedule, and closing date
- Details what's included in the sale (furnishings, appliances, etc.)
- Sets penalties for default by either party
- Requires a deposit (typically 10–30% of the purchase price)

The deposit is held in escrow or by your attorney until closing.

---

## Step 5: Pay the Transfer Tax and Close

At closing, you pay:

- **Transfer tax (ITP):** 3% of the property's assessed value (usually the purchase price)
- **Stamp duty:** Minimal, under $100
- **Attorney fees:** 1–2% (already engaged)
- **Registration fees:** ~0.5% to register the new certificate of title

Total closing costs for a foreigner: **approximately 4–5% of the purchase price.**

On a $300,000 property, expect $12,000–$15,000 in closing costs — significantly lower than the US (where 5–10% is typical).

---

## Step 6: Register the New Title

Your attorney submits the transfer documents to the Registro de Títulos. Within 30–60 days, a new Certificado de Título is issued in your name. This is your proof of ownership — the equivalent of a deed in the US.

## Taxes to Know

**Annual property tax (IPI):** 1% per year on the portion of value exceeding approximately DOP 9.5M (~$155,000 USD at current rates). If your property is worth $300K, you pay 1% on ~$145K = ~$1,450/year. Properties in tourism zones may qualify for IPI exemptions under Law 158-01.

**Capital gains on sale:** 27% of the profit. However, primary residences are exempt. If you're using the property as an investment/rental, budget for this on exit.

**No wealth tax.** No estate/inheritance tax for foreigners (though consult your home-country tax advisor).

## Can Buying Property Get You Residency?

Yes. Law 171-07 grants permanent residency to foreign nationals who invest at least $200,000 USD in Dominican real estate. The process takes approximately 45–90 days and gives you the right to live and work in the country. Meridian can connect you with immigration attorneys who specialize in this process.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Skipping the title search.** Always verify the title before signing a deposit.
2. **Paying in cash without a paper trail.** Use wire transfers with documentation for tax purposes.
3. **Not using a bilingual attorney.** Make sure your notary speaks your language or work with a translation-capable firm.
4. **Buying in an informal development.** Look for properties with registered titles (not just verbal land promises).
5. **Overlooking HOA fees and community costs.** Gated communities like Cap Cana have significant monthly HOA fees — factor them in.
